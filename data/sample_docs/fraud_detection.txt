Credit-card fraud detection is an extremely imbalanced problem, because fraud is well under one percent of all transactions. Accuracy is therefore a poor and misleading metric: a model that predicts no fraud at all is more than ninety-nine percent accurate while catching nothing. Better metrics are the precision-recall AUC, recall at a fixed precision target, and a calibration curve. Because the classes are so skewed, the model's probabilities should be calibrated so that a predicted score reflects a real likelihood of fraud. The decision threshold should not be left at the default of 0.5; instead it is chosen from a cost model. Missing a fraud, a false negative, is usually far more expensive than a false alarm, a false positive. Setting the cost of a missed fraud much higher than the cost of a false alarm pushes the threshold lower, so the system catches more fraud and sends borderline cases to manual review.
